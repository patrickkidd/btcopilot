## Statement 2194 — Robert in the Basement/Yard

**Text (preceded by AI question: "Where was your father, Robert, while all of this was happening?"):** "He was present physically, but wasn't really there. Robert usually stayed in the basement or out in the yard. If things got too loud with Clara, he'd just find a reason to leave the room."

**Codings:**
- Patrick: Distance
- Kathy: Distance
- Laura: Distance
- Guillermo: Distance + Projection (Sylvia→Claire; mother focused on/moved toward child, father distanced — child-focused triangle pattern)

Guillermo: I coded projection because I see the prototypical child-focused triangle — female moves toward child, male gets overwhelmed and distances. The others captured the distance half but not the projection half. "We're both capturing half of it."

Patrick: That's the reciprocity implied in the triangle. The distance and the projection are two sides of the same pattern.

Laura: For this statement, the only thing explicitly stated is the distance. The projection was more contained in the previous statement. You'd have to know theory to assume the projection is present here.

Patrick: This gets at a fundamental question — do you get ahead of what's explicit in the data using theoretical presuppositions, or do you stay with what's said?

Laura: I was assuming the distance was an anxiety reaction — anxiety up, functioning down by distancing.

Patrick: That's interesting. Are you coding anxiety up alongside distance?

Laura: I interpreted his leaving as anxiety up and functioning down.

Patrick: That raises the key question — do we code anxiety separately from relationship mechanisms? In all anxiety-binding mechanisms, you assume anxiety is present. Distance, over-functioning, conflict — all imply anxiety is being managed. So if you code distance, anxiety is already inferred.

Laura: My view: the anxiety variable is most informative when it's NOT a chronic/baseline phenomenon. When it's a discrete shift — someone dies and chronic anxiety goes up, or a storm, or an acute event — that's when anxiety as a variable is doing useful work. The chronic repetitive patterns have anxiety baked in.

Patrick: But they're technically separate variables that should vary independently. If they always co-occur, the theoretical hypothesis is just confirmed. But for the data to be falsifiable, you have to be able to have one without the other. What's the counterexample? Maybe Robert leaves for a hearing-structural reason with no emotional component — that would be physical distance without anxiety-driven distance.

Laura: Right. Physical distance isn't necessarily a threat reaction.

Kathy: Would that be different from the underlying chronic anxiety that's part of relationship patterns versus more acute anxiety — the flailing kind?

Patrick: There is an actual difference. Neurologically and endocrinologically, threat response is a different circuit — older evolutionarily, steers perceptual system, what you attend to, how sophisticated your response is. It's different from chronic anxiety that's built into functioning patterns.

Laura: In the long run, it will have more clarity to NOT code anxiety for each use of a relationship mechanism. Chronic repetitive patterns = baseline phenomenon. The anxiety variable is more informative for discrete, acute shifts.

Patrick: Maybe the convention is — maybe the first time you identify the pattern (first time we become aware of Robert's reaction to baby crying), you put anxiety in to establish that it's defined as anxiety-driven distance. But for every re-mention, you don't code anxiety again — that's just deduplication.

Laura: Anxiety is only informative when it's not a baseline phenomenon, unless it's a shift to a new baseline.

Patrick: There's nothing wrong with always coding two variables together — that just means the theory is correct and there's a high correlation. But they have to be falsifiable. You have to be able to have 0.1% of cases where they're not related, otherwise it's not scientifically testable.

Patrick: This is one of those things we can leave open and see how it evolves. It'll come up again with more examples and a convention will emerge.

**Resolution:** No rule established. Explicitly bookmarked as unresolved open question — anxiety implicit in R mechanisms vs. coding it explicitly. To be revisited with more examples.
