## Cross-Cutting Themes

**1. Cumulative Timeline as the Primary Comparison Unit**

Patrick announced a significant methodological insight: the cumulative timeline is what matters for comparison, not statement-by-statement agreement. He explained this emerged from both the IRR work and the AI extraction work: "Even when I'm having AI extract these in the personal app, it does better when you just take the whole discussion." Statement-by-statement data entry is still necessary for workflow purposes, but "the actual review needs to happen at the cumulative timeline level. It's like, all that matters is we came up with the same timeline." This partially resolves the measurement approach question (per-statement vs. cumulative) that had been open since Meeting 1.

**2. AI Calibration Tool — Underlying Construct Detection**

Patrick demonstrated a new calibration tool that takes all four coders' entries for a discussion and attempts to identify the "underlying construct" or shift that the coders were all trying to capture, even when coded differently. He described this as "pretty reliable" — citing the over-functioning example where all coders were trying to capture the same shift but coded it at different statements and with different codes. The tool organizes disagreements by these detected constructs and can rank them by priority, variable, or chronological order. Patrick noted this supports the earlier observation (from Meeting 2) that coders agree on the same shifts existing but code them at slightly different statements.

**3. AI Literature Review Tool — Per-Event Validation**

Patrick showed a per-event literature review tool — a button on each coded event that runs an AI prompt against the Bowen literature review database. For each code, it:
- Evaluates alignment with operational definitions from the literature
- Identifies specific textual markers from the case statement that align with literature definitions
- Links to specific passages in Family Evaluation and other sources with page references

The group tested it on several codes:
- **Over-functioning (Sarah):** Tool agreed, citing FE 218 ("person who feels responsible for the emotional well-being of others") and FE 224 (exhaustion from over-functioning, frustration that others aren't contributing). Patrick found the alignment "pretty good."
- **Distance (Michael):** Tool sided with distance over conflict, citing FE 681 ("husband becomes quite reactive to what is now experienced as excessive distance") and FE 677. It characterized Michael's "avoiding it all" as "cessation of the fight through withdrawal" — the opposite of engagement required for conflict.

**4. AI-Generated Language — "Emotional Insulation"**

The tool produced the term "emotional insulation" as a descriptor. Laura flagged this: "emotional insulation is not a concept that I'm familiar with." Patrick identified it as AI-generated language. Laura suggested it might mean "emotional isolation" which she'd have more familiarity with. Kathy noted she'd seen the term in Kerr. Patrick confirmed this was AI inference — "it kind of makes sense" — but emphasized the tool is "food for thought," not authoritative. The group discussed whether to constrain AI language: Laura asked "can we allow the AI to have that latitude?" Patrick said it's not worth restricting: "there's so many layers that it's not worth it." The traceable data set (lit review passages with page numbers) is what matters — users can go find the passage and make their own call.

**5. Distance vs. Conflict — Literature Tool's Position**

The tool analyzed the Sarah-Michael dynamic and sided with distance: "Michael's behavior, avoiding it all, is the opposite of engagement. It is the cessation of the fight through withdrawal." It cited Bowen's definition that conflict is "a state where neither gives in to the other and maintains emotional engagement." This directly engages the conflict vs. distance debate from Meetings 1-2. Laura noted this aligns with her position on the definitional boundary but observed "stepping back" has a dual meaning — it can describe anxiety-driven distancing OR thoughtful detachment/objectivity, which is "a more thoughtful thing than an anxiety-generated thing."

**6. Deduplication Rule — Laura's Catch-Up**

Laura (absent from Meeting 2) was brought up to speed on the deduplication rule. Patrick reiterated: "When somebody says something, like, when she says in one statement 'it frustrates me when I see him not trying,' and then if she says that in another way in the next statement, you should only add the delta for the first statement and don't repeat it after that. Because it stacks up in the cumulative timeline." Laura asked for clarification: "Would that apply just to that turn of talk, or all subsequent utterances?" Patrick: "All statements." Laura confirmed understanding.

Kathy raised her weight study observation again: repeated mentions might indicate emotional salience — if someone says "I'm so anxious" 70 times, the frequency itself is data. Patrick distinguished: don't re-code the same thing (that's the deduplication rule, "not up for debate for this system"), but Kathy's observation about frequency = importance is a separate valid insight. He agreed the frequency information is meaningful but shouldn't be captured through duplicate deltas.

**7. Blind Coding Protocol — Lit Review Tool Timing**

The group debated whether coders should use the per-event lit review tool before or after completing their blind coding. Key positions:
- **Kathy:** Suggested treating the AI as another blind coder — don't look at its analysis until after your own coding is complete. "That seems the cleanest if we're actually doing an IRR."
- **Laura:** Agreed with Kathy's position.
- **Patrick:** Initially ambivalent ("I kind of decided to just not worry about it"), then agreed it's a choice about training materials. He framed it: the lit review tool is scoped context (like looking at Bowen's book), not a fellow coder's output, but conceded Kathy's point is cleaner for IRR purposes.
- **Kathy:** Further distinguished: "This is training right now, and at some point when it would not be training, it would be an actual research evaluation. That would be a time to decide."
- **Group consensus:** Keep blind for now during training — code first, then use the lit review tool after. Decide the protocol for formal research later.

**8. SARF Variables — A and R Are Distinct**

The AI calibration tool produced the analysis: "SARF distinguishes between internal experience of stress and the relational mechanism of overfunctioning used to manage the stress within the family system." Laura questioned whether this was accurate. Patrick explained: it's inferring from the data model — "you track shifts for one person at a particular point in time. And you have separate variables which literally means they're supposed to vary independently." He noted the AI derived this correctly without being explicitly told, which he found impressive.

**9. R Variable as Non-Ordinal**

Patrick clarified a data model distinction: S, A, and F can be drawn as line graphs (they increase and decrease — ordinal). R shifts "just kind of happened" — they're events, not levels. In the personal app, R is shown as a blue vertical line on the timeline rather than a curve. This connects to Laura's earlier weight study observation: they coded "there was a shift" without specifying which kind, which is lower resolution but captures the essential information.

Patrick outlined the big picture: Round 1 cases (Sarah, Marcus, Jennifer) are now treated as practice. Round 2 cases (Arthur, Dominic, Cassandra, Robert) have improved AI conversational flow with more nuance. He directed everyone to code Arthur blind for the next meeting (March 30). Patrick has already coded Arthur, Dominic, and Cassandra. Kathy advocated for Marcus or Jennifer instead (since she'd preserved her Sarah coding per the "don't change reviewed data" rule and made paper notes only), but Patrick explained the tools review doesn't require comparing codes — it's about the tools themselves. Laura hasn't done Marcus or Jennifer.

Guillermo described the Arthur case: "The AI will put a theme in there that you won't notice was there the whole time, but you'll pick it up 15-20 statements down the line. It's really cool. It's like listening to real conversations."

## Tools Review: Sarah Case Codes

*Rather than statement-by-statement deliberation, the group reviewed specific codes through the AI tools.*

### Over-Functioning (Sarah)

**Patrick's code reviewed:** Over-functioning — Sarah exhausted by mom's confusion, Michael doesn't help.

**AI tool analysis:** Aligned with operational definition. Markers:
- "I feel like I'm the only one holding things together" → FE 218: "person who feels responsible for the emotional well-being of others"
- "It's just exhausting, honestly" → FE 224: exhaustion/overwhelm from over-functioning
- "Michael doesn't really help much" → FE 224: frustration that others aren't contributing

**Laura's observation:** FE 224 says the over-functioning person "derives a sense of well-being by virtue of doing for others" — Patrick and the group noted this wasn't evident from Sarah's statement. The AI may have been drawing from the broader passage context rather than the specific quote.

**Group assessment:** Tool got the concept right; citation accuracy approximately "half right" — it found the right passages but sometimes mapped claims to wrong sub-sections within a passage.

### Distance (Michael — highest priority disagreement)

**AI tool analysis:** Sided with distance over conflict. "Michael's behavior, avoiding it all, is the opposite of engagement. It is the cessation of the fight through withdrawal." Citations from FE 681, 677.

**Laura's reaction:** Noted "stepping back" is ambiguous — can mean anxiety-driven distancing OR thoughtful detachment. "We often talk about detachment or objectivity, stepping back from the emotionally reactivity... that's kind of a more thoughtful thing than an anxiety-generated thing."

**Tool reliability note:** Patrick found the tool was linking to wrong events in some cases — a bug. The summaries were sometimes correct conceptually but linked to the wrong coder or wrong statement.
