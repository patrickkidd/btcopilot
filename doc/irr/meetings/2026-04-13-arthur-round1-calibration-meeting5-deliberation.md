## Cross-Cutting Themes

**1. Structural Events vs. SARF Shifts — Formal Distinction**

Patrick articulated a clarity he'd been developing: all events divide into two categories — structural and SARF (functional). Structural events change the family configuration: birth, death, marriage, divorce, bonded, moved, adopted. SARF shifts track how the organism functions in response. "Structural events are not SARF shifts. A birth is not an SARF shift, and neither is getting married or divorced or death." You track structural events on the timeline, then code SARF shifts that occur in response to those events — potentially at the same timestamp but as separate data entries. This aligns with the data model design: the add-event form only allows one type per entry. Laura endorsed: "Structure and function. Really interesting." Patrick noted this distinction matters because structural coding has relatively clear rules (e.g., add parents, add pair bonds) while SARF coding "requires a lot more debate, and that's where all the substance for the IRR process is."

**2. Functional Facts / Lifetime Markers — Gap in the Data Model**

Laura identified a significant gap: occupation, education, health, and geographic location are markers Bowen defined for tracking multigenerational functioning, but there's no place for them in the current coding system. This came up because Kathy and Guillermo both entered "school teacher" as a shift event for Claire, which Patrick flagged as note-taking mode rather than a shift. Laura explained: "Those kind of functional facts — occupation, education, health — were specifically defined by Bowen as markers of functioning. Particularly important in trying to track multigenerational functioning across generations." She proposed a possible solution: a "baseline marker" category under functioning, with subcategories for occupation, education, health, location. Patrick noted the person and event notes fields in the Pro app serve this purpose currently, but acknowledged "there's got to be a way to get something in the note fields from here." Laura emphasized these are "very much part of the methodology of the family diagram — the original methodology" where you write notes next to people and get a holistic snapshot. Both agreed this is a longer-term data model question, not something to solve now. Patrick: "We can do without those for our purposes at the moment."

**3. Symptom Definition — Narrower Than Previously Used**

The group made significant progress on the symptom vs. anxiety distinction. Kathy had coded "symptom up" for Sylvia's sensitivity to disruptions, influenced by the Meeting 4 discussion where symptom was used broadly for presenting problems. Laura pushed back firmly: "It would be better not to widen the definition of symptom, to leave it as anxiety. Because that would be more consistent with theory." Her reasoning: sensitivity is a characteristic of a person, part of natural variation in reactivity — it's anxiety, not symptom. For something to be a symptom, there must be functional interference — analogous to how drinking isn't a symptom unless it interferes with functioning "physiologically, behaviorally, or socially."

Patrick agreed: "I would have left symptom out" and said he's "trying to just always pull them apart" (keep the SARF variables distinct). He then offered a parallel from engineering: failure mode and effects analysis (FMEA). Symptom = failure mode (how the system breaks down). Anxiety and functioning = etiological (causal). Relationship = systemic-level etiology. "Symptom occurs when anxiety exceeds functioning by too much" — it's the breakdown point, not the sensitivity itself.

Laura found this framing useful: "Anxiety and functioning have to do with etiology. Symptom would be if there's a breakdown." She mapped it: relationship variables are at the level of the relationship system, then individual anxiety and functioning are affected by one's position in that system, and symptom is the failure point when "anxiety exceeds functioning by too much."

Kathy acknowledged: "I loosened up my criteria for symptom" and would revert to the narrower definition.

**4. Baseline vs. Shift — Timestamp Requirement**

Laura introduced a critical distinction: coding a SARF shift requires a timestamped event, not just a baseline characterization. "My mother has always been sensitive to disruptions" describes a baseline level of functioning, not a functional shift. In order to code functioning down, "it has to be kind of a time-stamped event." Patrick initially coded functioning down for this but reconsidered: "that's actually interesting." Laura's position: you can't code a shift from a description of how someone "always" was.

However, the group found a nuance: "When Claire was born, that shift became permanent." This IS a timestamped shift — from fluctuating sensitivity to permanent baseline. Laura conceded: "It could be" a shift to a new basic level. Patrick: "So there was more fluctuation, and then it just became the baseline. She never, I guess, it never went away. So I think that could be a shift down to a basic level. None of us got that."

Related: for baselines that are just "the way it was," use the "unknown" date certainty field. Code once, don't duplicate.

**5. Over-Functioning Confirmed — Automaticity and Choice**

Laura was the only coder who put over-functioning for Arthur on the "kept things quiet at home" statement. Patrick agreed upon review: "I'll give you that, I agree with that." He reiterated his over-functioning definition from previous meetings: "Whether or not you had a choice to do it differently" — distinguishing over-functioning (automatic, emotionally driven) from simply helping because it's needed (reality-based). "When is it over-functioning versus just picking up the slack because it's a good idea?" Laura endorsed: "There's a thoughtfulness in a contact with reality, an objectivity. One's reality-driven, one's emotionally driven." Patrick: "And then that would be the rule for all the mechanisms, I guess" — the reality-driven vs. emotionally-driven distinction applies across all relationship mechanisms.

**6. Chronic Patterns vs. Episodes — Deduplication Refinement**

Laura raised whether chronic patterns should be coded multiple times. Her position: if a father goes to the garage to avoid, and it happens at different times in response to different events (e.g., "when Evelyn got sick, he really retreated"), each episode with a different timestamp is a valid separate entry. But the baseline pattern itself ("he always retreated to the garage") gets coded once. Patrick agreed: "Different episodes of over-functioning" at different timestamps are legitimate separate entries. Baseline descriptions that are repeated across statements should not be duplicated. This refines the deduplication rule: it's not about how many times something is mentioned, it's about whether distinct time-dated episodes exist.

Laura had gone through her Arthur coding before the meeting to "prune" duplicate entries, keeping ones that represented distinct episodes and removing ones that were just restatements of the same baseline.

**7. Coding Functioning Down for Infants**

Laura had coded symptom up and functioning down for Claire as a colicky baby. Patrick questioned coding functioning down for an infant: "Their neocortex is like part of their cerebellum." Laura agreed the functioning down doesn't belong for a baby. The symptom (colic) is valid; the functioning assessment isn't meaningful for an infant.

**8. Bowen's Variables of Differentiation — 30 Not 27**

Laura found a reference in a 2022 Family Systems Journal article (by Catherine Rackauer) about Bowen's development of the differentiation concept. The article includes a sidebar noting that Bowen mentioned "30 variables to differentiation" in a Pennsylvania lecture — updating the "27 variables" figure cited by Laura in Meeting 4. The variables may be in the Bowen archives. Laura: "She said, I'd like to know what they are. I think they're in the archives." Patrick: "We can define them ourselves."

### Rules Established

- **Symptom requires functional breakdown.** Sensitivity, reactivity, and behavioral characteristics are anxiety, not symptom. Symptom = failure mode — the point where anxiety exceeds functioning capacity. Do not widen symptom to include presenting behaviors or personality characteristics.

- **Don't code functioning for infants.** Colic can be coded as symptom but functioning assessment is not meaningful for infants.
