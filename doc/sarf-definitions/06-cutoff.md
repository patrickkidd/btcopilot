## Operational Definition

*Synthesized exclusively from the passages indexed below. Each claim is traceable to specific source passages. No AI training data used.*

**Cutoff** (Emotional Cutoff) refers to the way people manage the unresolved emotional attachment to their parents and families of origin by reducing or totally cutting off emotional contact with them. It is extreme, chronic distance across generations.

Cutoff is distinguished from distance by its intensity, chronicity, and intergenerational focus. While distance may fluctuate and be relatively easily reversed, cutoff represents a more fixed state of emotional separation that a person carries through life.

**Key characteristics:**
- Cutoff is a way of managing undifferentiation, not resolving it
- The emotional attachment remains beneath the cutoff; intensity is suppressed, not eliminated
- Cutoff can be physical (geographical separation, no contact) or emotional (superficial, ritualized contact)
- The more intense the cutoff from family of origin, the more intense the emotional process in the nuclear family
- Cutoff increases vulnerability to stress and symptoms
- Cutoff is replicated - the child who cuts off from parents tends to have children who cut off from them

**A HIGH cutoff** is indicated by:
- Little or no contact with extended family members
- Highly charged, negative descriptions of family members
- Inability to discuss family without intense emotional reaction
- Denial of importance of family relationships
- "Declaration of independence" that is really flight, not growth
- Patterns of running away from family at times of stress

**A LOW cutoff** is indicated by:
- Maintained contact with extended family across generations
- Ability to discuss family members with emotional neutrality
- Comfort with visiting family without being destabilized
- Realistic assessment of family strengths and weaknesses
- Contact that is genuine, not just ritualized obligation

## Key Discriminators

### What Cutoff IS (positive definition)

1. **Cutoff is a way of managing undifferentiation between generations** - Not resolving it, just managing it through avoidance (FE9-21)

2. **Cutoff does NOT resolve the emotional attachment** - "Declaring one's 'independence' from family is not differentiation of self. He has broken away but not grown away" (FE9-22)

3. **The old attachment remains latent** - Can be revived with emotional contact; the intensity is suppressed, not gone (FTiCP9-10, FTiCP9-11)

4. **Cutoff exists on a continuum** - "Individuals and nuclear families are cut off emotionally from their extended families in degrees" (FE9-19)

5. **Cutoff can be physical OR emotional** - Physical separation, OR present but emotionally distant with superficial contact only (FTiCP9-10)

6. **Cutoff increases nuclear family intensity** - Spouses cut off from extended families are more dependent on each other; emotional process more intense (FTiCP9-11)

7. **Cutoff increases vulnerability to symptoms** - Loss of extended family support and stabilizing force (FE9-19, FE9-20)

8. **Cutoff is replicated across generations** - Pattern transmits from one generation to next (FE6-133, FE6-155)

### What Cutoff is NOT (negative definition)

1. **Cutoff is NOT differentiation** - Breaking away is not growing away; the fusion remains unresolved (FE9-22)

2. **Cutoff is NOT healthy independence** - It's emotionally reactive avoidance, not thoughtful autonomy (FE9-22)

3. **Cutoff does NOT eliminate the relationship** - The connection persists beneath the surface; shows up in dreams, reactions, sensitivities (FTiCP9-10)

4. **Cutoff does NOT protect from anxiety** - May reduce anxiety in one area but increases it in another (FE9-20)

5. **Physical distance is NOT automatically cutoff** - One can be geographically distant but emotionally connected; or geographically close but cut off (FTiCP9-10)

6. **Resolving cutoff does NOT mean becoming dependent on family** - Goal is emotional contact with objectivity, not fusion (FE9-23)

## Cross-Reference Analysis

### Convergence Across Sources

FE Ch 6, FE Ch 9, and FTiCP Ch 9 agree:

2. **Cutoff increases nuclear family intensity** - Spouses more dependent on each other (FTiCP9-11)
3. **Cutoff can be physical or emotional** - Both achieve same avoidance function (FTiCP9-10)
4. **Cutoff replicates across generations** - Children tend to repeat pattern (FE6-133)
5. **Bridging cutoff reduces symptoms** - Improved contact reduces anxiety and symptoms (FE9-24, FE9-25)
6. **Cutoff exists on continuum** - Degrees of cutoff, not binary (FE9-19)

### Tensions/Nuances Between Sources

1. **FE Ch 9 emphasizes symptom connection** - Cutoff as major vulnerability factor
2. **FE Ch 6 emphasizes triangle dynamics** - Cutoff affects how triangles operate
3. **FTiCP Ch 9 emphasizes nuclear family intensity** - Cutoff concentrates process in marriage

## Gaps and Uncertainties

1. **Cutoff threshold** - At what point does "distance" become "cutoff"? Sources describe continuum but don't define threshold.

2. **Intentional cutoff** - When someone cuts off from abusive family, is that different? Sources don't address "justified" cutoff.

3. **Partial cutoff** - Can one be cut off from some family members but not others? How to code?

4. **Cutoff resolution timing** - How quickly can symptoms change when cutoff is bridged? Sources say "dramatic" but don't specify.

5. **Generation specificity** - Is cutoff only from family of origin, or can it apply to siblings, adult children?
