## Key Finding: High Stochasticity

Repeated runs on the **same prompt** showed anxiety F1 varying between 0.000 and 0.014. This variance makes targeted optimization extremely difficult - changes that appear to help or hurt may just be noise.

## Error Patterns Analyzed

1. **Anxiety Over-Extraction**: AI codes 47.6% of events with anxiety vs GT's 12.5% (4.5x over-extraction)
2. **General Emotions as Anxiety**: AI codes resentment, guilt, grief, sadness as anxiety when GT doesn't
3. **Description Mismatch**: Even when both have anxiety events, descriptions differ enough to not match
4. **Missing anxiety DOWN**: GT codes "sense of relief" as anxiety DOWN; AI rarely codes DOWN

## Root Cause Analysis

The anxiety F1 problem appears to be structural rather than prompt-solvable:

1. **GT has only 11 anxiety events** across 74 cases - very sparse signal
2. **F1 matching requires similar descriptions** - AI and GT describe same events differently
3. **Model sensitivity**: Any change to anxiety definition causes model to stop using the field entirely
4. **Stochastic behavior**: Same prompt produces different results on repeated runs

## Key Changes Made

None retained. All 5 iterations were reverted to baseline.

## Recommendations

⚠️ **F1 plateaued at 0.014**: Anxiety extraction is near random chance given:
- Sparse GT signal (11/88 events = 12.5%)
- High model variance between runs
- Sensitivity to any prompt modification

**Suggested next steps**:

1. **Schema-level changes**: Consider if anxiety should be a separate extraction target rather than an event field
2. **More GT data**: 11 anxiety events is too sparse to train on effectively
3. **Description standardization**: GT and AI use different phrasings; consider fuzzy matching in F1 calculation
4. **Temperature/sampling**: Reduce model temperature to decrease run-to-run variance
5. **Focus on other variables**: Relationship F1 (0.014) and functioning F1 (0.014) may be more tractable
